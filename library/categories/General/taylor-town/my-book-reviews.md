Zen and the Art of Happiness
----------

This book repeats itself: "convince yourself that you're living in the best possible universe".

The Prime Number Conspiracy
----------

Good variety of math journalism hits with that pleasing Quanta style.

Soulhome
----------

My first foray into progression fantasy. Cool magic system propping up a weak plot.

Good Inside
----------

Dr. Becky is the best! Great guide to navigating big emotions in parenting and life.

Factfulness
----------

A great case that the world is getting better and that human intuition is broken.

Give and Take
----------

Unremarkable inspo porn.

Bullshit
----------

Surprisingly deep philosophical musings on BS.

An Elegant Puzzle
----------

A collection of concrete wisdom about engineering management.

How Innovation Works
----------

This book traces the paths of how tinkerers improve ideas. I think I preferred Isaacson's *Innovators*, which illustrated many of the same ideas more clearly.

Musashi
----------

Epic.

The Mom Test
----------

Brilliant guide to validating ideas.

Motivational Interviewing
----------

This book contains life-changing strategies for active listening.

The Golden Compass
----------

Awesome. I somehow missed reading this as a child, but it's filled with depth for adults.

Selfish Reasons to Have More Kids
----------

This book makes an excellent case that most people should consider having more kids. In many ways, the central argument is "Kids make people happier. Most parents spend way too much time on individual children. Nature will eventually dominate your nurture, so nurture less and make more nature."

It's written by an economist; the research was comprehensive and convincing. The parenting advice was, uh, obviously written by an econimist.

Three Laws of Nature
----------

Potent primer on energy & entropy. Very focused, no filler. Highly recommended.

Sirens & Muses
----------

A book about art students that seems to both idolize and resent its subject matter. I suspect I would've enjoyed this book more if I were an actual artist.

Creation Lake
----------

There is so much to love about this story. The Neandrathal stuff was very thought-provoking. Unfortunately, the plot lost way too much momentum in its midsection, and the payoffs were not sufficiently setup to my tastes.

Ministry of Time
----------

Charming book. I would've preferred a short story.

Complexity: A Guided Tour
----------

A scattered (but very entertaining) survey of complex systems. Sometimes felt like reading a "greatest hits" of all the coolest ideas in math/science, and that's okay.

Supercommunicators
----------

It's like HTWFAIP written by Malcolm Gladwell! And I really mean that in the best possible way -- I thoroughly enjoyed this book, and feel like I learned a lot from it.

How Evil Are Politicians?
----------

Too much Bryan Caplan for me!

The Spider's War
----------

Satisfying conclusion to a fantastic series.

The Case Against Education
----------

This book mixes flamboyant hubris with intellectual humility -- I think I actually quite enjoy those flavors together: strong opinions, supported with meticulous evidence, but hedged with solid arguments against your case.

But is education really "80% signaling"? Yeah, I think I'm pretty convinced of that. But still unsure what to do with that information though. If it's true, there's a vast and undervalued market gap for talent search/certification.

Radical Abundance
----------

Decent crash course into atomically-precise manufacturing. Great frameworks for thinking at small scales. Drexler seems to have a beef with the science community, which felt like a constant distraction from the brilliant ideas in the book.

How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk
----------

This book really should be called "How to Talk So *People* Will Listen". It's loaded with actionable advice that I've already started using with my family.

The Widow's House
----------

Okay, I really just need to know what happens next. Good writing, great plot, but just a lot of it.

A Naked Singularity
----------

A "perfect" book. Unbelievably, indescribably good. It's hard not to compare it to DFW's fiction; both the writing style and its subject matter evoked *Infinite Jest* and *Broom of the System*, and -- blaspheme incoming -- I much preferred *A Naked Singularity*. The plot, the themes, the characters, the feels, the research -- what an experience!

A General Theory of Oblivion
----------

A potent literary tangle.

The Impossible Man
----------

This biography must've been exceptionally difficult to write. How does an author explain generational harm without excusing it? How do they cover the pursuit of genius without encouraging some of its most negative stereotypes?

The Tyrant's Law
----------

I am loving this series! …and I want it to move faster.

The Road
----------

A book about life, death, and evil. It'll remain in your memory like a scar. Highly recommended.

Prisoners of Geography
----------

An easy introduction to contemporary geopolitics. I wish I had read this in middle school!

The King's Blood
----------

Great fantasy! Rarely do I read sequels, and even more rarely do I enjoy them this much.

Cadillac Desert
----------

It's one of those non-fiction books that changes everything. Rarely do I read something that needs to take such provocative measures to explain things that should obvious and uncontroversial. Water is essential.

Welcome to the Hyanam-dong Bookshop
----------

This book is the product of many positive social movements. While I didn't enjoy the book's plot all that much, I'm very happy to see the zeitgeist carry us here!

Thinking with Type
----------

A gorgeous, delightful, informative book. This work takes the art of printed words to another level.

James Acaster's Guide to Quitting Social Media
----------

Acaster is funny, as usual; the book achieves some surprising depth through absurdism.

Fab
----------

A decent book about a powerful idea: the imminent personal fabrication revolution. So hungry for more details/depth; I'll probably need to watch the related "How to Make Almost Anything" course online.

Drive Your Plow Over the Bones of the Dead
----------

Probably very good, but too much for me for now.

Tyll
----------

Nice, dark folklore.

Glucose Revolution
----------

Practical guide to managing blood sugar, backed by science.

The Idiot
----------

This novel is so detailed, it's hard to believe it's not a memoir. It's sometimes too smart but overall beautifully written.

I admittedly stopped reading this book after \~75% completion. The plot evoked too many memories of longing, humiliation, regret, etc.

The Dragon's Path
----------

Excellent fantasy! I'd put it somewhere between Brandon Sanderson and George R R Martin. Great characters, intriguing plot, well-crafted twists.

Oblivion: Stories
----------

To be honest, it's a bit too much David Foster Wallace. Most of the stories are brilliant; some are extraordinary chores.

The Dream Machine
----------

An opinionated-yet-measured history of personal computing via the lens of JCR Licklider, "Computing's Johnny Appleseed".

Heaven
----------

Cool exploration of good/evil via manic pixie dream girl. Clunky execution overall.

The Employees
----------

Objectification! Of androids, of humans, of women, of men, of employees, of objects, etc. Very well done.

Convenience Store Woman
----------

This tiny book made me feel a lot of things. It made me… wistful? kinder? weirder? resentful?

Nexus
----------

In short: information is neutral, bureaucracy is interesting, and computers are powerful. As usual, Harari is an excellent writer, but I felt somewhat underwhelmed by this book. I suspect that this book was written to convince other people of these things.

Capitalist Realism
----------

Makes a solid case that capitalism is responsible for much of the malaise and misery of our era. I wish the book made clearer descriptions of alternatives to capitalism. I understand that that was not the point of the book, but as the book says, it's really hard to even picture a world without capitalism at this point.

No Country for Old Men
----------

One of those rare books with incredible characters, beautiful (yet unpretentious) prose, gripping plot, clever dialogue, and enough depth to drown yourself.

The Strangest Man
----------

Cool biography about cool dude.

Trust
----------

It's a metafictional novel about capital/power with a deeply compelling plot! Wow!

Pastoralia
----------

One of the best fictional works I've ever encountered. Funny! Painful! Rich! Otherworldly!

Atonement
----------

I generally have no patience for stories like this. I don't know how this differs from other romance/WWII books, but, wow, *Atonement* hits hard in all the right ways at all the right times -- an expertly crafted plot.

When We Cease to Understand the World
----------

I adore Labatut's willingness to take liberties with history; it's the perfect amount of speculation injected into non-fiction. Stories of mad geniuses are irresistible to me, and this book is full of such true(ish) tales.

The Friend
----------

Well-written literary fiction, but nothing remarkable.

Hateship, Friendship, Courtship, Loveship, Marriage
----------

Tidy, enjoyable short stories.

The War of Art
----------

If you need inspiration or a swift kick-in-the-butt, read this book. Since reading, I've been unable to make my usual excuses that divert me from my art and career and health. For me, every page contained a mixture of timeless wisdom and awful advice. This book is overall charming and pragmatic.

God's Debris
----------

From few axioms, *God's Debris* forms a surprisingly coherent religion, philosophy of science, and practical life advice; a worthwhile \~90-minute read.

Interview with the Vampire
----------

Imagine *The Count of Monte Cristo* meets *Faust* meets *Twilight* -- it's an epic tale with deep philosophical underpinnings… and vampires! This book is worth reading if you want to dramatically ponder death, love, and evil.

Norwegian Wood
----------

This book should be labeled with an extreme trigger warning. It covers death and sex and more death and more sex and a whole lot of depression. Imagine blending together *Catcher in the Rye*, *The Great Gatsby*, *The Perks of Being a Wallflower*, *Scott Pilgrim*, and a smut novel. I think I learned a lot about selfishness and happiness from this experience. I wish I read this when I was 19.

The Way of Kings
----------

If Sanderson's *Mistborn* is *The Chronicles of Narnia*, then *The Stormlight Archives* is *The Lord of the Rings*. It's magical, political, and finely crafted. Brandon Sanderson knows how to write payoffs. This book alone contains 383,000 words, so clear your schedule before reading.

The Handmaid's Tale
----------

Much of *The Handmaid's Tale* is surely beyond my comprehension, but it provided a sobering glimpse into some of the unfairnesses of nature and society. Atwood's general take on United States fundamentalism is outstanding. The book is riveting throughout, but its ending secures it a "modern masterpiece".

Animal Farm
----------

*Animal Farm* delightfully illustrates how authoritarian regimes usurp control. Although the book seemed to be specifically a critique of Stalin's Russia, there are gems to glean about perverse institutional incentives.

The Colour of Magic (Discworld #1)
----------

This was my first Terry Pratchett book. Oh boy, what a delight! If you like clever authors like Raymond Smullyan and Douglas Adams, you'll adore his writing. *The Colour of Magic* covers some surprisingly deep ideas about science and religion wearing a witty grin.

The Remains of the Day
----------

*The Remains of the Day* is a subtle story from the perspective of a charming butler. I highly recommend this book if you're in a wistful mood or receptive to deep introspection.

The Stranger
----------

This book is very highly acclaimed. After reading it, I thought I missed something, so I read a few critical analyses -- nope, I understood it perfectly. *The Stranger* has a decent plot with great writing. This book was probably ground-breaking in 1942, but existentialism has been beaten to death since then. On my end, this is probably a case of ["Seinfeld is unfunny"](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/SeinfeldIsUnfunny).

Metamorphosis
----------

*Metamorphosis* is potent. It's witty, horrific, deep, and bizarre -- an unforgettable experience.

Discipline is Destiny
----------

This book was inspiring but forgettable. All the examples of disciplined people got me *pumped*, but I didn't find much actionable content.

Ubik
----------

Good sci-fi stories explore "cool ideas". *Ubik* miraculously interleaves 3-5 "cool ideas". And it keeps topping itself with bigger and bigger extensions of its core "cool ideas". Read *Ubik* if you like "cool ideas".

Triggers
----------

This is a self-help book that delivers. It's an incredibly useful guide on how to enact lasting change in yourself. This book focuses on growth via environmental cues. I particularly liked the concrete advice on daily active questions, accountability partners, and actionable ways to change cognitive contexts.

Book of the New Sun
----------

These books are extremely subversive, yet the author falls prey to the exact tropes he's "subverting". The plot oscillates between mind-bending self-reference and painful self-awarelessness. Some of the twists are incredible. I suspect that this is an excellent book that aged poorly.

Solaris
----------

A sci-fi book that gave me literal nightmares. It's unsettling and thought-provoking.

The Good Earth
----------

A heartbreaking rags-to-riches-to-rags story about wealth, family, work, fairness, kindness, and *land*. It's easy to see why a book about rural Chinese peasants was the best-selling book in the US during The Dust Bowl and The Great Depression. This book will inspire or haunt you.
