Last weekend, I went to the National Gallery of Scotland. This time, I started my trip on the bottom floor where all the Scottish art is on display. A few minutes after entering the gallery, I was captivated by paintings of Nature and of Edinburgh. I noticed that one wall was dedicated to breathtaking paintings of Nature, whereas on the other there were monumental, detailed cityscapes.

I started looking at one painting on one side, then zig-zagging to the other side. City and country, side-by-side. Then I looked out one of the windows between two cityscapes. The window looked out onto Princes Street Gardens, the park in the heart of Edinburgh. Outside, too, was city and Nature side-by-side. It felt special being able to look at paintings and then, next to them, see a glimpse of the city they depict. The art is close to its subject.

When I go an art gallery and see a painting, I am regularly in awe. Some paintings catch my eye to an extent that I stand there for minutes looking for details. There is a term for this: slow looking. To look slowly is to focus on what is in front of you for a few minutes. Some details reveal themselves only with time and study. I revel in all the details I notice – the colour of the sky, the expressions on figures’ faces, how objects in the painting relate, and anything else I see. I love listening out for what other people talking about a painting say too; others’ perspectives help me see an artwork in a different light.

The more I look, the more I see. The details are right there in front of me, but they take time to see. I don’t see everything in a painting when I see it first. I have seen the paintings of Nature and cityscapes that I had seen last weekend a few times before, but this time I noticed how they all fit together – Nature and city side-by-side. A particular painting of a waterfall caught my eye. No matter how many times I go, I always see something new. I feel awe.

This morning, I realised that the root of visual analysis, a skill in art history, is noticing – to pay attention to a piece of work and see what you see. I also realised this relates to my work with technology. I spend a lot of time looking at websites and studying them – their purpose, design, writing style, navigation, use of typography.

When I notice something is done in a particular way, or see a pattern applied that I have only studied, or study a painting I have seen but didn't understand at the time, I feel a little moment of joy. I feel joy both at having noticed something, and in the thing I have noticed.

My study of the web and design, like my study in art history, is enhanced by learning what to look for – through study and practice and learning from others, I can notice more. This is one point in which two of my interests – art and technology – meet: in both, noticing is applied directly.

Noticing is the foundation of many of my writings, too, now I think of it; and the foundation of so many other areas of life, too. It took this blog post for me to think of things that way. Writing is how I communicate what I notice. I wonder if any of the painters I have studied felt the same way.
