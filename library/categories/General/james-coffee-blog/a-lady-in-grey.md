I *love* thinking about the use of colour in painting. Impressionist paintings never cease to make me smile; their colour illuminates me as it does the places they depict (oh! how wonderful art can be!). I also love paintings that use grey and white, both sparingly and as part of a broader theme.

[*A Lady in Grey*,](https://www.nationalgalleries.org/art-and-artists/5122) which I saw at the National Gallery of Scotland this weekend, depicts the daughter of the artist, Sir. Daniel Macnee, sitting outdoors in a grey dress. When I first saw this painting, in my peripheral vision, I stopped and thought *I need to go up for a closer look!* I felt a curiosity like I did when I first saw Picasso’s [*Fernande with a Black Mantilla*](https://jamesg.blog/2024/09/23/fernande-with-a-black-mantilla), another portrait that makes use of grey.

Here is A Lady in Grey:

![](https://s3-eu-west-1.amazonaws.com/ngs-mw-prod/9/27/9729/9729.jpg)

The sitter – the lady – looks straight ahead, immediately getting the attention of the viewer. She is sewing. The spool of white material is at the bottom of the sitter’s grey dress. Although there is nothing to imply it, the spool had me thinking about the passage of time. I don’t yet have the words to articulate why.

The folds in the sitter’s dress remind me a bit of sculpture; they are all so carefully detailed.

In the background – dark in contrast to the light tones in which the sitter and her clothes are painted – there is a forest of trees; there is a path behind the lady. I find forest scenes like this relaxing – the sitter is seemingly at peace with her sewing as she spends the day outdoors. I love how the sun shines through the tops of the trees onto the path in the background — the detail is terrific.

Alongside the painting in the gallery is [the study made in preparation of the larger work](https://www.nationalgalleries.org/art-and-artists/5132). I enjoyed looking back-and-forth between the paintings, noticing how the rough outlines and use of light and shadow in the preparatory painting are reflected the final painting.
