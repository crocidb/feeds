A while ago, I purchased an [Aranet 4](https://aranet.com/en/home/products/aranet4-home) indoor CO2 monitor. The device has sat on my desk ever since. I started thinking about the Aranet monitor this week because I noticed that, for the first time, the battery was low and needed to be changed.

The Aranet is a useful device. When the number is high on the device, it means I probably haven’t opened a window in a while; CO2 has built up in the room due to a lack of fresh air circulating through. I often go and open the window when I notice. It also measures temperature. As I write, it is 13.1 C in the room. I know I should turn the heating on, but I am one of those people who is like “I’ll put it on later.”

The Aranet mostly fits into the background. This makes me wonder: why does this piece of technology recede into the background?

First, there are no buttons. The device reports on CO2, temperature, and something else (humidity I think?). I can’t interact with it beyond reading. Second, the display is e-ink, so it doesn’t capture my attention. The device does flicker when it updates which, as I write this blog post, I am noticing more, but it is always the case that when you start to look at something you notice more. For the most part, though, the device is static.

The Aranet is also silent to the extent I understand. It doesn’t encourage me to open the window by playing a chime, but it does give a subtle indicator on screen when the CO2 levels are getting high. I appreciate that the Aranet doesn’t try to get me to open the window because, as noted earlier, it can get quite chilly and I don't want to open the window if I am in a room that is cold already. In this way, the device solely gives me information, then leaves me to make a decision.

The Aranet has an application that I tried out. I am sure there are lessons to learn from that technology too but, after trying it, I realised I didn’t really need the graphs or data. I get the most value out of the device by seeing the number on the screen; it doesn’t need to be connected, nor does it ask me to connect.

I watched an excellent [talk from Amber Case on “Designing Calm Technology”](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=clxm5qW3pao). When I have time I am sure to look through some of the papers on the Calm Technology website; [work on the idea of “calm technology” goes back to Xerox PARC](https://calmtech.com/papers/designing-calm-technology). I think “calm” is an adequate descriptor of the Aranet.

When I broaden my mind to include non-digital tools in my definition of technology, I realise I am surrounded by tools that I *can* do something with beyond reading (unlike the Aranet), but that don’t ask for my attention. My pencils sit on the desk until I use them (or, indeed, fidget with them, as I do!).

Related to the ideas of calm and background technology I have been thinking about the term “ambient technology”. I suppose ambient and background are closely connected now that I think about it; technology that exists in the background but is there when you need it.
